Task: B2
## Discover 5 Unique Strong Security Implementations

### Description

This task involved researching and documenting five real-world examples of strong, effective security implementations. Each example is drawn from publicly documented practices, frameworks, or deployments in 2024–2025. The goal was to identify what made each implementation robust, how it worked in practice, and what lessons it offers.

### Findings

#### Finding 1 — Google BeyondCorp: Zero Trust Architecture at Enterprise Scale

**Security principle:** Zero Trust — never trust, always verify

Google developed BeyondCorp starting in 2011 in response to nation-state attacks (Operation Aurora) against Silicon Valley companies. Rather than relying on a trusted internal network perimeter, BeyondCorp enforces access controls at the application level regardless of the user's physical location or network. Every access request — whether from a Google office or a coffee shop — is treated as potentially untrusted and must be authenticated and authorised based on verified user identity and device posture.

**Key implementation details:**
- All corporate applications are served over the public internet, not behind a VPN
- Access is granted based on identity (who you are) and device state (patch level, encryption status, certificate validity) — not network location
- The system uses a central Access Proxy that enforces policy before any request reaches an application
- MFA and continuous device health checks are mandatory

**Why it is strong:** BeyondCorp eliminates the dangerous assumption that anything inside the network can be trusted. Even if an attacker gains access to the internal network (e.g., via a compromised contractor), they still cannot access applications without a verified identity and compliant device. Google has used this system for over a decade at scale, and it became the model for NIST SP 800-207 (Zero Trust Architecture) and the US federal government's Zero Trust mandate.

**Source:** Google Cloud — *BeyondCorp: A New Approach to Enterprise Security*; Wikipedia — *BeyondCorp*.

#### Finding 2 — FIDO2 Passkeys / Phishing-Resistant MFA (Microsoft Entra ID)

**Security principle:** Phishing-resistant authentication using asymmetric cryptography

FIDO2-based passkeys replace passwords and traditional OTP-based MFA with a cryptographic credential bound to a specific device and origin (website or application). When a user registers a passkey, the device generates a public/private key pair. The private key never leaves the device; only the public key is stored on the server. During authentication, the device signs a server-generated challenge with the private key, and the server verifies it using the stored public key. Because the credential is cryptographically bound to the exact origin URL, it cannot be replayed against a phishing site.

**Key implementation details (Microsoft Entra ID):**
- Passkeys are supported via Windows Hello, the Microsoft Authenticator app (iOS/Android), and hardware security keys
- Conditional Access policies can require phishing-resistant MFA for privileged roles or sensitive applications
- The private key is stored in secure hardware (TPM or Secure Enclave) and never transmitted over the network
- Microsoft is rolling out Entra passkeys on Windows to general availability in mid-2026

**Why it is strong:** Traditional MFA (SMS OTP, push notifications) can be bypassed via real-time phishing proxies. FIDO2 passkeys cannot — the credential only works for the exact origin it was registered with. The US federal government's OMB M-22-09 Zero Trust strategy required 100% phishing-resistant MFA across federal systems by 2024, citing FIDO2 and PIV as the only acceptable methods at the highest assurance level.

**Source:** Microsoft Learn — *Passkeys (FIDO2) in Microsoft Entra ID*; FIDO Alliance — *Passkeys overview*; eMudhra blog on phish-resistant MFA.

#### Finding 3 — US Federal Zero Trust Mandate and CISA Implementation (2022–2025)

**Security principle:** Government-mandated security baseline with structured accountability

In 2022, the US federal government issued a Zero Trust strategy (OMB M-22-09) requiring all federal agencies to achieve specific Zero Trust security goals by the end of FY 2024. CISA (the Cybersecurity and Infrastructure Security Agency) published a Zero Trust Maturity Model to guide agencies through implementation. The mandate explicitly required: mandatory MFA across all systems, network segmentation, encryption for internal traffic, robust logging and continuous monitoring, and phishing-resistant authentication for privileged accounts.

**Key implementation details:**
- CISA partnered with OMB to track and audit agency progress against five pillars: Identity, Devices, Networks, Applications, and Data
- GSA and the Department of Health adopted FIDO2 standards for phishing-resistant authentication
- Agencies were required to maintain secure configuration baselines and document their Zero Trust maturity annually

**Why it is strong:** Unlike voluntary frameworks, this mandate created legal and regulatory accountability for security controls. By encoding Zero Trust principles into federal law and tracking implementation through CISA's maturity model, it shifted agencies from reactive to proactive security postures. As of 2025, federal agencies have been making measurable progress, and the framework has become a reference model for critical infrastructure sectors.

**Source:** CISA — *Zero Trust Architecture Implementation Report* (January 2025); DHS publication; Northern Technologies Group analysis.

#### Finding 4 — End-to-End Encryption with Post-Quantum Cryptography Readiness (Industry-Wide, 2025)

**Security principle:** Defence-in-depth through encryption and crypto-agility

In response to the "harvest now, decrypt later" threat — where adversaries collect encrypted data today to decrypt it once quantum computers become capable — leading organisations have begun migrating to post-quantum cryptography (PQC) algorithms approved by NIST in 2024. The NIST-approved algorithms include ML-KEM (for key exchange) and ML-DSA/SLH-DSA (for digital signatures). A January 2025 survey of 600 security professionals found that 81% of organisations were prototyping or evaluating PQC adoption.

**Key implementation details:**
- Organisations are inventorying all cryptographic assets (libraries, certificates, keys) to identify where legacy algorithms (RSA, ECDH) are in use
- Hybrid key encapsulation mechanisms (KEM) in TLS 1.3 allow organisations to use both classical and PQC algorithms simultaneously during the transition
- PCI DSS 4.0 (enforceable from March 31, 2025) and EU DORA (in effect January 17, 2025) now require strong encryption and documented cryptographic governance

**Why it is strong:** Proactive migration to PQC addresses a long-term, systemic risk before it materialises. Organisations implementing crypto-agility — the ability to rapidly swap cryptographic algorithms — are building resilience against both current and future threats. This is a forward-thinking implementation rather than a reactive patch.

**Source:** Semnet — *Zero Trust and Encryption Best Practices 2025*; FIDO Alliance; Thales 2025 Data Threat Report

#### Finding 5 — Continuous Bug Bounty Programs as Proactive Security (HackerOne / Bugcrowd Model, 2025)

**Security principle:** Crowd-sourced, continuous vulnerability discovery before attackers

Organisations running mature bug bounty programs engage a global community of ethical security researchers to continuously test their systems and responsibly disclose vulnerabilities in exchange for financial rewards. In 2025, this has evolved from occasional one-off exercises into always-on, deeply integrated programs. Bug bounty teams are now embedded within internal security and development workflows, with automated ticketing and direct communication channels between external researchers and engineering teams.

**Key implementation details:**
- Programs like HackerOne, Bugcrowd, and Intigriti facilitate structured disclosure with legal protection for researchers
- Scope typically covers production web applications, APIs, mobile apps, and cloud infrastructure
- Critical vulnerabilities often attract bounties of $10,000–$100,000+, incentivising high-quality research
- According to Omdia (2025), organisations increasingly launch programs proactively before product launch rather than only after a breach

**Why it is strong:** Bug bounty programs provide access to a diverse, global pool of security expertise that internal teams cannot replicate. They surface vulnerabilities in pre-production and production environments continuously, meaning flaws are found by friendly researchers rather than hostile attackers. The model also counteracts the ransomware incentive model by creating a financial reward structure for responsible disclosure.

**Source:** Dark Reading / Bugcrowd — *Bug Bounty Programs Rise as Key Strategic Security Solutions* (2025); Intigriti blog; Omdia *Executive Summary: Bug Bounty Programs* (2025).

### Reflection

Researching strong security implementations revealed that the most effective approaches share three properties: they remove implicit trust rather than simply adding controls on top of it; they make attacks economically and technically impractical rather than merely difficult; and they are continuous rather than periodic.

Google's BeyondCorp is the clearest example of removing implicit trust. By treating the internal network as equally untrusted as the public internet, it eliminates the single point of failure that perimeter-based security creates. The Change Healthcare breach (documented in B1) is the direct counterexample — a trusted internal network assumption combined with a missing MFA control proved catastrophic.

The FIDO2 passkey implementation is particularly compelling because it addresses phishing at the cryptographic level rather than the human level. Attempting to educate users to spot phishing pages is a losing battle; binding a credential to a specific origin means there is nothing for users to get wrong.

What surprised me most was the federal Zero Trust mandate. Government cybersecurity is often perceived as slow-moving and compliance-focused, yet the US mandate created genuine structural accountability for security outcomes rather than just checkbox compliance. The mandate's emphasis on measurable maturity levels and cross-agency collaboration offers a model that private sector organisations could adopt internally.

The post-quantum cryptography work stands out as the most forward-looking implementation — organisations are spending resources now to address a threat that may not materialise for 10–15 years. This reflects a mature security culture where decisions are made based on long-term risk modelling rather than only immediate threats.
