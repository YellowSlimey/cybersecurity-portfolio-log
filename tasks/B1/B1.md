Task: B1
## Discover 5 Unique Weak/Vulnerable Security Implementations

### Description

This task involved researching and documenting five real-world examples of weak or vulnerable security implementations discovered in practice. Each example is drawn from publicly disclosed incidents between 2024 and 2025, with verifiable sources. The goal was to identify the specific security failure, its consequences, and the underlying weakness class.

### Findings

#### Finding 1 — Missing MFA on a Public-Facing Remote Access Portal (Change Healthcare, 2024)

**Weakness class:** Missing authentication control

The BlackCat/ALPHV ransomware group breached Change Healthcare — a UnitedHealth Group subsidiary that processes 15 billion healthcare transactions annually — by logging into the company's Citrix remote access portal using stolen employee credentials. The portal had no multi-factor authentication (MFA) enabled. Attackers gained initial access on 12 February 2024 and moved laterally for nine days before deploying ransomware on 21 February 2024, exfiltrating 6 TB of data in the process. UnitedHealth CEO Andrew Witty confirmed in congressional testimony that the absence of MFA on a publicly accessible Citrix portal was the root cause.

**Impact:** Approximately 190 million Americans had sensitive health data (names, SSNs, medical records, billing information) exposed, making it the largest healthcare data breach in US history. UnitedHealth paid a $22 million ransom and incurred over $3.1 billion in direct breach-related costs.

**Source:** BleepingComputer — *Change Healthcare hacked using stolen Citrix account with no MFA* (2024); UnitedHealth CEO Congressional testimony.

#### Finding 2 — No Enforced MFA Across 165 Customer Cloud Accounts (Snowflake Breach, 2024)

**Weakness class:** Absence of enforced authentication controls at the platform level

The cloud data platform Snowflake did not enforce MFA for customer accounts by default. Threat actor group UNC5537 (also linked to ShinyHunters and Scattered Spider) harvested credentials from infostealer malware logs dating back to 2020 and used them to perform credential-stuffing attacks against Snowflake customer instances. Because Snowflake had not required MFA, and because customer accounts lacked IP allowlisting, the attackers needed only a valid username and password to log in undetected.

**Impact:** At least 165 organisations were compromised, including AT&T (over 50 billion call and text records stolen), Ticketmaster, Santander Bank, Neiman Marcus, and Advance Auto Parts. The operation generated over $2 million USD in extortion payments.

**Source:** HackTheBox blog — *Exploring the Snowflake Breach (Attack Anatomy)*; Wikipedia — *Snowflake data breach*; Cloud Security Alliance analysis (2025).

#### Finding 3 — Unpatched Zero-Day in File Transfer Software Exploited at Scale (MOVEit / Cleo, 2024–2025)

**Weakness class:** Unpatched known vulnerability / failure to apply security updates

The Cl0p ransomware group exploited a zero-day vulnerability (CVE-2024-55956 and related flaws) in Cleo's managed file transfer platform in late 2024, enabling unauthenticated remote code execution. Organisations using the software had not patched within the available window. This followed a near-identical pattern from the 2023 MOVEit breach, where Cl0p exploited CVE-2023-34362 in the MOVEit Transfer product to compromise over 2,700 organisations worldwide and expose data belonging to over 93 million individuals. Despite high-profile coverage of MOVEit, many organisations repeated the same mistake with Cleo.

**Impact (Cleo):** Hertz, Dollar, and Thrifty brands disclosed a breach affecting over 1 million customers (April 2025). **Impact (MOVEit):** 93 million individuals affected across government, healthcare, and financial sectors.

**Source:** PKWARE — *Recent Data Breaches* (2025); ManageEngine blog — *The security gaps that caused 2025's biggest breaches*.

#### Finding 4 — Stolen Credentials with No Rotation or Dark Web Monitoring (AT&T / Snowflake Supply Chain)

**Weakness class:** Poor credential hygiene and absence of credential monitoring

In the AT&T/Snowflake breach, investigators found that the stolen credentials used in the attacks had originated from infostealer malware infections dating as far back as 2020. The credentials had appeared in criminal underground markets and stealer logs for years before being exploited. AT&T had not implemented dark web credential monitoring to detect exposed employee credentials, nor had it enforced credential rotation policies. No network-level IP allowlisting was applied to limit where the Snowflake accounts could be accessed from.

**Impact:** AT&T's Snowflake environment was accessed, exposing call and text metadata for nearly all cellular and landline customers — approximately 50 billion records — covering a period from March 1 to October 31, 2022.

**Source:** SecureFrame — *Biggest Data Breaches of 2024*; HackTheBox — *Snowflake Breach Attack Anatomy*.

#### Finding 5 — Insider Threat via Overseas Contractors with Excessive Data Access (Coinbase, 2025)

**Weakness class:** Excessive access privileges / inadequate insider threat controls

Coinbase disclosed in May 2025 that overseas customer support contractors had been bribed or coerced into leaking customer data beginning on 26 December 2024. The incident exposed names, contact details, partial Social Security numbers, masked banking data, and ID images. The root weakness was that third-party support contractors had been granted access to sensitive customer records beyond what was necessary for their roles — a violation of the least-privilege principle. Coinbase faced a $20 million extortion demand from the threat actors.

**Impact:** Sensitive customer PII exposed, significant reputational and regulatory exposure, and $20 million extortion demand. Coinbase declined to pay and estimated remediation costs of up to $400 million.

**Source:** PKWARE — *Recent Data Breaches 2025*; Coinbase official disclosure blog.

### Reflection

Researching these five cases revealed a striking pattern: the most damaging breaches of 2024–2025 were not the result of novel or sophisticated zero-day exploits, but rather the failure to implement basic, well-understood security controls. Three of the five cases (Change Healthcare, Snowflake, AT&T) could have been prevented entirely by enforcing MFA — a control that costs little and has been industry-standard advice for over a decade.

What stood out most was the scale of harm enabled by a single missing control. The absence of MFA on one Citrix portal at Change Healthcare exposed 190 million people's most sensitive health records and cost over $3 billion. This illustrates that weak security implementations do not just create technical risk — they create cascading, systemic harm at a societal level.

These findings also highlight how attackers exploit the gap between when patches are released and when they are applied. The MOVEit and Cleo breaches demonstrate that Cl0p actively monitors for newly disclosed vulnerabilities and weaponises them immediately, leaving very little time for organisations to respond. Proactive vulnerability management and automated patching are essential, not optional.

Finally, the Coinbase case reinforced the importance of insider threat modelling. Strong perimeter security is undermined if internal or third-party users are granted more access than their role requires. The principle of least privilege must extend beyond employees to contractors and outsourced support functions.
